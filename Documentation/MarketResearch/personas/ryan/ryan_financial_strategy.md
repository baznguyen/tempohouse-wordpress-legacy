## Gap Analysis

### Business Gaps
- No booking or reservation system exists — revenue is leaking at the moment of conversion. Every interested party who cannot book online either books a competitor or does not book at all. For private events — the highest-margin product — an enquiry form is the only intake mechanism. There is no deposit collection, no commitment device, and no CRM to track the pipeline. This is a structural cash flow problem disguised as a website problem.
- Private event hire is described as the highest-margin revenue stream, yet it has received the least commercial infrastructure investment. There is no event package pricing published, no minimum spend policy, no case studies or past event gallery, and no dedicated sales outreach to corporate event planners, brand agencies, or marketing teams — the three buyer categories with budget and urgency.
- Revenue is almost entirely walk-in and word-of-mouth. There is no owned audience: no email list, no newsletter, no loyalty mechanism. With zero email subscribers, zero CRM, and zero tracked repeat visits, the business cannot forecast demand, cannot fill slow periods, and cannot sell private events proactively. Every revenue day starts from scratch.
- No analytics or tracking is confirmed live on the website. The business has no data on how many people visit the site, which pages they land on, where they drop, or whether anyone has ever submitted the events enquiry form. Operating without this is the equivalent of running a business without a till receipt — you cannot manage what you cannot measure.
- Unit economics are unknown and untracked. Without a reservation system, there is no data on average covers per service, average spend per head for café vs bar, or event revenue per hire. Without this, there is no basis for pricing decisions, staffing decisions, or identifying which of the four revenue streams (café, bar, gallery program, events) is pulling its weight versus draining it.

### Website / Digital Gaps
- No online booking — the website cannot take a reservation, collect a deposit, or confirm an event enquiry with a commercial commitment. It accepts a contact form submission and then goes silent. This is the single largest conversion failure on the site.
- No event CMS — there is no backend system for publishing, updating, or removing events. Every event update requires a developer. This means the events calendar cannot be kept current, which means it will go stale, which means visitors will stop checking it. A stale events calendar is worse than no events calendar because it signals operational neglect.
- No Vietnamese language content — 80 to 85 percent of HCMC hospitality searches are in Vietnamese. The website addresses precisely none of this audience in their language. For a venue that names its bilingual Aussie-Vietnamese founding identity as a differentiator, this is a commercial contradiction: the brand claims cultural duality but serves only one language.

The website is invisible to the discovery mechanisms that Vietnamese and expat audiences actually use. A physical venue that does not appear on Google Maps is commercially invisible to the majority of potential customers who search before visiting.

## Tempo Rhythm Strategy

**Headline Concept:** Sell the same room four times a day at four different price points to four different buyers — and never let any of them feel like they are sharing a product.

**Business Implications:** The tempo rhythm concept, when commercialised correctly, is not just a marketing story — it is a yield management strategy. A venue that successfully segments its day into four distinct commercial products with different buyers, different prices, and different margin profiles has materially better revenue resilience than a single-format venue of the same size. The café provides daily cash flow and customer acquisition. The bar provides higher per-head margin and extends dwell time. The gallery programme builds credibility and generates press that reduces customer acquisition cost across all formats. Private events generate the highest gross margin and the most predictable cash flow once the pipeline is built. The dependency chain is: photography enables editorial, editorial enables discovery, discovery builds audience, audience fills the café and bar, the café and bar demonstrate the room in use, and the room in use sells the private events. Every piece connects. The business is not failing because the concept is wrong — it is failing because the conversion infrastructure has not been built yet.

### Implementation Ideas
- Price each tempo as a distinct commercial product, not a time slot. The morning café, the gallery afternoon, the evening cocktail service, and the private event hire each have different margin profiles, different customer acquisition costs, and different willingness-to-pay. Treat them as four separate revenue lines in the P&L, not four modes of one business. This changes every downstream decision: how you staff, how you price, how you market, and how you measure whether the business is working.
- Create a 'Full Day' private hire product at a significant premium — the client buys the entire building for a full day arc (morning breakfast briefing, afternoon gallery viewing, evening cocktail reception). This is the highest-margin product TEMPO can sell and it directly monetises the 'different tempos throughout the day' concept as a client experience. Corporate teams doing offsite strategy days and brand agencies running client immersion days are the buyer. Price it at VND 80-150M for a full day with minimum spend. One booking per week at this price is transformational for the P&L.
- Build the events calendar as a commercial engine, not a content feature. Each event in the 'What's On' section should have an explicit revenue model attached: ticketed entry, minimum bar spend, gallery acquisition enquiry, or venue hire conversion. A gallery opening with 80 people standing at the bar is a bar service event. An art talk with 40 people and an artist present is a community-building event that drives gallery acquisition and press. Know which is which and price accordingly.
- Sell the tempo shift itself as a product for corporate clients. Brand activation agencies pay premium prices for venues where the aesthetic transforms during the event — a brand launch that moves from a morning coffee reveal to an evening cocktail celebration in the same building, with the art on the walls changing the visual context, is an extremely compelling pitch. No other venue in HCMC can offer this. Package it, photograph it, and take it directly to brand marketing managers at FMCG, luxury, and tech companies operating in Vietnam.
- Use the time-of-day operating model to smooth revenue across the day and reduce the café/bar peak-trough problem. Introduce 'gallery afternoon' programming (artist talks, collector previews, creative workshops) between 14:00-17:00 — the slowest café period. These events have low production cost, build the cultural community, generate press, and give the gallery programme commercial content. Ticket them at VND 150,000-300,000 per person or bundle with a drink. Twenty people at an artist talk on a Tuesday afternoon at VND 200,000 each is VND 4M in revenue from a time slot that would otherwise generate close to zero.

Options in order of speed: (1) Sanity or Contentful headless CMS connected to the existing Next.js site — 1 week to implement; (2) WordPress CPT for events managed via the existing WP install — 3 days. Without this, the events calendar goes stale within weeks.
- Online reservation system — a table booking capability that captures party size, date, time, name, and contact, sends an automated confirmation, and creates a record. Minimum viable: integrate Tock or Resy (both work in Vietnam) in 2 weeks. The custom reservation system is a 6-month build — do not wait for it. Get a SaaS solution live now and migrate later.

This alone improves conversion on the highest-margin product.

## Additional Recommendations

- Run a P&L by revenue stream immediately — even with estimated numbers. Assign a portion of the fixed cost base (rent, utilities, core staffing) to each revenue stream proportionally (café, bar, gallery, events). This will reveal whether the café is actually profitable after rent allocation, whether the bar's margin justifies its hours, and whether the private events product is being priced correctly. Without this analysis, the business cannot make a rational decision about where to invest limited capital or where to cut. I would not be surprised if the café is running below breakeven on a per-square-metre basis and the events product is dramatically underpriced.
- Implement a deposit policy for private event hire immediately — a minimum 30 percent deposit on booking, non-refundable within 7 days of the event. This is standard industry practice and serves two commercial purposes: it confirms genuine intent (eliminates tyre-kickers from the pipeline) and it creates a cash inflow event at the point of booking rather than at the point of delivery. For a business with a debt problem, cash timing matters as much as total revenue.
- Consider a 'founding member' or pre-opening programme if TEMPO is not yet at full trading. A limited cohort of 50-100 founding members who pay a nominal upfront fee (VND 1-2M) in exchange for priority reservations, early event access, and a named recognition in the venue generates immediate cash, builds the founding community, and creates social proof before the venue has earned reviews. This model works for early-stage venues carrying debt because it converts future intent into present cash.
- The gallery art commission model needs to be defined and made explicit. If TEMPO is hosting rotating exhibitions, there should be a formal commission arrangement with exhibiting artists on any works sold during the exhibition period (industry standard is 40-50 percent). This turns the gallery programme from a cost (curation time, wall space) into a revenue stream with zero production cost. Even one artwork sold per exhibition at VND 10-30M changes the economics of the gallery programme materially.
- Do not launch the custom reservation system before securing the first 10 private event bookings. The research into the reservation system is excellent and the architecture is sound, but it is a 6-24 month build that will consume significant development resource. The commercial priority in a debt situation is revenue velocity, not infrastructure completeness. Get a SaaS reservation tool live in two weeks, get the B2B events pipeline moving, and plan the custom build as a Phase 2 investment funded by the revenue it will help generate.
